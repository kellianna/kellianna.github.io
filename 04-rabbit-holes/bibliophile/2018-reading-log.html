January 2018

A really good beginning to a year of reading! In January I read:
.

  1. Between Breaths - Elizabeth Vargas - A memoir of anxiety and addiction. It was ok. It felt very rushed like too much was crammed into it.
    .
  2. ‎The Fault in our Stars - John Green - YA novel about a romance between two teens with cancer. I devoured it. Very well written tear-jerker.
    .
  3. ‎Umbrella Academy - Gerard Way (graphic novel) - I'm not one for comic books usually, but was curious about this because of the upcoming Netflix series. It was dark and weird and I can't decide if I want to read the next one or not. I didn't hate it.
    .
  4. ‎The Heart of Haiku - Jane Hirshfield (Kindle single) - A short book about the haiku master Basho. A lovely read including many of his haiku.
    .
  5. ‎The Red Tent - Anita Diamant - I read this 20 years ago and had completely forgotten what happened in it. A biblical story, but not a religious story. Speaks to the power and strength of women. Now one of my top 3 books.
    .
  6. ‎Hard Times Require Furious Dancing - Alice Walker - A quiet, thoughtful collection of poetry. I will definitely return to these again and again.
    ...
    I also started: @aluxaard 's novel/poetry/memoir, "The Alux Chronicles." Very good so far! Sharon Salzberg's "Real Love" about metta meditation and learning to love with an open heart. "Presto!" by Penn Jillette about how he lost 100 pounds. Very entertaining! Finally, "If You Want to Write" by Barbara Ueland. Not my favorite book on writing so far. Yes. I'm always reading multiple books at once. It's been a lifelong thing.

February 2018

February book roundup:

  1. Presto!: How I Made Over 100 Pounds Disappear and Other Magical Tales - Penn Jillette - I really enjoyed Penn’s writing, even if he is a bit of a name dropper. This book is about much more than just how he lost 100 pounds after having a heart attack and being told he should have weight reduction surgery. The diet he used is quite extreme and maybe a little crazy. An enjoyable read even if you don’t need to lose weight!

  2. How to Stop Time - Matt Haig - I really loved this book about a man who ages very slowly and has lived hundreds of years. It’s a bit far fetched that he ran into several well known people in those years, but the encounters fit the story well. It captured my interest and by the end I wondered how it would all come together with just 30 pages to go. I’ve read a couple of other Haig books and they’re not intellectual, literary reads. They’re feel good books that offer palatable insights into life. Haig has been very open about how writing saved him from a dark, suicidal place. His books are easy, uplifting and fun and sometimes we all need that.

  3. Tattoos and Time - Wren Kay - Beautiful collection of poetry focusing on nature and sensuality from our own @wrenpoetess. I really loved the feel of these quiet poems and will definitely reread it again and again!

  4. Romeo and Juliet - William Shakespeare - I assigned this to my daughter for school, so read it with her. I read it as a freshman in high school and don’t remember it being so racy. I surprised myself at how well I could understand the flowery language.

  5. Alux Chronicles - Michael Sanders - I started this one last month and finished this week. I have been a fan of @aluxaards poetry since I started on Instagram and really enjoyed this poetry collection/memoir. So much so that I’m going to order a hard copy after reading it as an ebook. This isn’t just a poetry collection. Michael bares his soul and bravely shows us moments of his life. The book shows the evolution of his poetry and his growth as a person. I highly recommend it!

I’m still reading “Real Love” by Sharon Salzberg. I’m taking that one slowly because there is a lot of work to do there. I also started reading “Theft by Finding” the diaries of writer David Sedaris. I love it! He is so human and hilarious!

March 2018

March Book Roundup:

  1. Theft By Finding - David Sedaris - I really enjoyed this collection of diary entries from 1977 - 2002. Knowing how much success he’s had as an author, it’s interesting to look back on his days of being poor, writing at his local IHOP, doing construction work, and his heavy drinking and drug use. The book was funny and insightful and made me think differently about the way I keep a journal. Sedaris doesn’t just write about his day to day life, but collects little bits of dialogue and scenes he observes. I’ve been trying to do more of that in my own journal.

  2. Animal Farm - George Orwell - I assigned this book to my daughter for school, so I reread it as well. Definitely a classic that lives up to the hype. Well written and eye opening. I realize it’s supposed to be about the dangers of communism, but I could see a lot of parallels to our current political atmosphere. The gaslighting the pigs use on the other animals is something we’re seeing everyday from the White House.

  3. The Death of Roger Ackroyd - Agatha Christie - It’s been a stressful, crazy couple of months in our house, and my brain needed a break. I tend to buy these Agatha Christie e-books when they’re on sale for $2 so I can turn to them when I just want to cozy in with some brain candy. This one wasn’t as good as the others I’ve read. “And Then There Were None” is a wonderful book! This one was ok and the ending was a bit of a surprise!

  4. Me Talk Pretty One Day - David Sedaris - After reading his diaries, I wanted to read an actual Sedaris book. I realized I’ve had this one on my bookshelf for years! Funny little essays about the author’s life. Very entertaining and funny. I actually did laugh out loud a few times at his struggle to learn French.

I’m a little late with this, but my whole household has been suffering from colds and flu. Headaches, fevers, body aches, coughing, runny noses. I’m looking forward to warm weather so we can all get out and make some Vitamin D!
April 2018

Book Roundup:

  1. White Noise - Don DeLillo - I love DeLillo’s casual observations about life - the almost poetic, philosophical moments of reflection on human nature. I enjoyed the first half of the book, but by the end I became a little more unsure about the story. I really can’t say why, but he just kind of lost me. It is an unusual story and it made me think. I would still recommend it.

  2. The Fact of the Body - Alexandria Marzano-Leznovich - This was a very disturbing read based on the true story of the murder of a little boy in the early 90s combined with the author’s own experience of being abused as a child. It was a hard book to read, infuriating in the way the author wasn’t protected by her own parents, but well researched and well written.

  3. What Unites Us - Dan Rather - I’ll admit I’m a fan of Dan Rather probably because of his “liberal bias.” He lays out what he feels are the challenges facing the United States today and, calling on many historical moments, suggests what we need to do to fix things. I enjoyed the history lessons of the book, and while he does seem to gush about the past, he also balances it with the things that we haven’t always gotten right. I don’t think it left me with the optimism he was hoping for. It instead made me realize that the more things seem to change, the more they just really stay the same.

I didn’t do as much reading last month as I would have liked, but it was a tough month. I also can’t share a snazzy picture of my stack of books because I only read ebooks. It’s so convenient to borrow them from the library and have them delivered right to my Kindle. I really do enjoy the ritual of an actual book, but sometimes life calls for the convenience of an ebook. In addition to the books I finished, I also started reading “The Wind-Up Girl,” but could not get into and gave up on. I started a book I’m really enjoying called “The Abundance of Less “ about people in Japan who give up the rat race for a quiet life in the mountains. And I’m halfway through reading “Brave New World” which I assigned my daughter for school and we’re reading together.

May 2018

May Book Roundup

  1. I'll Be Gone In the Dark/Michelle McNamara - There is a lot of information packed into this true story of the Golden State Killer. McNamara researched every possible lead and I can imagine how the details weighted on her. The story of the victims and the pursuit by law enforcement is told in a very narrative style that drew me in completely. I wish she could have survived to see the killer caught and to have continued her pursuit of the real stories of other unsolved crimes. I highly recommend this book.

  2. Big Magic/Elizabeth Gilbert - I almost gave up on this one after the first chapter. When Gilbert starts talking about ideas floating around in the ether, landing in artist’s brains, and floating on to someone else if they don’t get used - I was ready to stop reading. But, I stuck with this book about writing and creativity and I ended up getting a lot of really good inspiration out of it in the end. The advice about not writing as a career, but as a joy and a passion was something I needed to hear. Skip this one if you’re already in a good place with your art.

  3. Brave New World/Aldous Huxley - Very mixed feelings. I know it’s a classic, but it’s not my favorite dystopian future novel. It seemed a bit all over the place. A very interesting set up that didn’t seem to really go anywhere. I did enjoy the message that there isn’t anything wrong with not going along with the crowd - the most interesting people never do! I found “1984” a much more interesting story as far as classic dystopian novels.

  4. Show Your Work/Austin Kleon - This is a very quick read on how to get your work out to the world in some very non-traditional ways. Kleon includes many, many quotes from other artists on sharing your work. Worth a read if you need ideas of getting your art out there.

  5. Swan/Mary Oliver - Love, love, love Oliver and I really enjoyed the poems in this very slim book. She just knows how to mix nature and the human spirit and she really speaks to me. Well worth reading!

June 2018

June Book Roundup

  1. Felicity/Mary Oliver - I love Mary, but this wasn’t my favorite collection of hers. There were some excellent poems, but overall it was a very short collection without anything that really pulled me in. Still, even here, she writes more beautifully than I can ever hope to.

  2. Buddhism and Zen/Nyogen Senzaki - I’m used to westernized, pop psychology Buddhism written in plain language. This was the real thing from the 40s by a zen monk from Japan. While I feel that I got the roots of what the book was saying, I was lost most of the time. I had it from the library so I hurried through. Perhaps the concepts would sink in more if I had more time with them.

  3. The Rain in Portugal/Billy Collins - This was the first time I had read a collection of poetry by Billy Collins and I really enjoyed it. I like the everyday feel of what he writes about. I like the seemingly plain language that hides something deeper. I will definitely be reading more from him!

  4. Reasons to Stay Alive/Matt Haig - This is part memoir of the author’s struggle with his mental health and part a primer on depression and anxiety. It was his story and the story of millions of others. I suffer from the anxiety end and could relate to much of what he experienced. This is an excellent book for anyone who is suffering from depression and anxiety or wants a better understanding of what that experience is like.

  5. An Abundance of Less/Andy Couturier - Set in Japan about people who leave the rat race of Tokyo for a life in the mountains. Many are artists and activists who protest the impact of nuclear power and urbanization. While I may not be ready to move off the grid and grow my own food, I found this book inspiring. They have chosen their own path in a country that rewards uniformity. They’re living their truth with very little material wealth. I felt like the book was almost a meditation on a simpler way of life.
    …
    I hadn’t let myself start anything new until I finished up everything I was already reading. I’m going to try to limit myself to 2 books at a time. We’ll see how long that lasts. I’m now reading “Bird by Bird” by Anne Lamott and “The Soloist” by Mark Salzman.
